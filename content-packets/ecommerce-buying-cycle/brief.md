# Ecommerce Buying Cycle Brief

## Working Angle

Small business owners often treat a Shopify store like a digital shelf: put products online, make the site look decent, run some ads, and hope people buy. But ecommerce is still sales. The store has to do the job a good salesperson would do in a physical shop: help customers find the right thing, answer questions, build confidence, remove risk, and ask for the sale.

This guide should explain the ecommerce buying cycle in a quick, practical way, using real Australian ecommerce brands as proof of each concept. It should feel like the mobile UX video: a clear opening, a small number of concepts, on-screen examples, practical commentary, then a checklist-style action for the viewer.

The lesson is not "learn marketing theory". The lesson is: grab your phone or laptop, load up your Shopify store, and check whether each step of the buying cycle is being handled properly.

The hook should use a bait-and-switch structure: start with an obvious statement the viewer agrees with, then add a reversal that creates tension. The point is not to insult small businesses; it is to say the quiet part out loud. Example: people do want to support local brands, but they still need the store to feel credible, safe, and easy to buy from.

Include an early note that the buying cycle affects messaging as well as page design. If you send the wrong message at the wrong time, you can kill a sale. For example, an abandoned cart discount is not always the right response. Depending on where the customer is in the cycle, they may need trust, delivery clarity, product education, sizing help, reassurance, or a reminder rather than a discount. Klaviyo, Shopify Email, Mailchimp, or any other email/SMS platform should be used to send the right message at the right time based on behaviour, not just blast generic offers.

Proposed title direction:

- The Ecommerce Buying Cycle: What Shopify Store Owners Need To Understand
- Why People Visit Your Shopify Store But Do Not Buy
- Your Shopify Store Is Not A Shelf, It Is A Sales Conversation
- How Customers Actually Decide To Buy From Your Shopify Store

## Target Viewer

- Small business owners running Shopify stores.
- Owners who may have started with a physical store, local retail business, market stall, or product brand.
- Operators who know their products and customers in person, but have not translated that sales knowledge into ecommerce pages.
- Australian store owners who want practical examples, not abstract funnel diagrams.

## John Positioning In This Guide

John should come across as the approachable translator between big-brand ecommerce practice and smaller Shopify stores.

The positioning is:

- "I work with large agencies and major Australian ecommerce brands, so I get to see how serious ecommerce teams think."
- "That does not mean I am claiming to be The Good Guys, Koala, or any other brand we inspect."
- "It means I can look at what big brands do well, explain the thinking behind it, and show smaller stores which parts are worth copying."

The emotional role is almost interviewer/advocate: ask the big-brand ecommerce questions on behalf of smaller store owners, then bring the useful answers back in plain English.

Use this lightly. The video should still be about the viewer's store, not John's resume.

Important proof angle for this guide:

- Ironically, brands like JB Hi-Fi and The Good Guys can feel more personable, safer, and more trustworthy online than many local stores.
- That is not because big brands are naturally more human. It is because they spend heavily and think deliberately about making the digital experience feel helpful, safe, and reassuring.
- Many small local stores unintentionally create a sterile online experience even though the real people behind the business are warm, knowledgeable, and trustworthy.
- The useful lesson is not "become a big brand". It is "copy the trust-building behaviours that make big brands feel safe online."

## Style And Structure To Match

Use the successful structure from the mobile experience guide:

1. Open with a sharp, relatable observation.
2. Make the physical-store comparison.
3. Name the small number of things we care about.
4. Move through each concept with real Australian store examples.
5. Say what the example is doing well, or where it could work harder.
6. Translate the example into a practical check for the viewer's own store.
7. End with a simple action: open your store and review it step by step.

The delivery should be conversational and fast-moving. Explain each concept quickly, then prove it with the store example. Avoid long definitions.

## Storytelling Rule

Use the Trey Parker/Matt Stone "but/therefore" principle for the video structure.

The script should not feel like:

- "Here is point one, and then point two, and then point three."

It should feel like:

- "Here is what the store owner believes, but here is why that breaks online. Therefore they need to handle this part of the buying cycle. But even if they fix that, another risk appears. Therefore the next part of the store has to do this job."

Do not literally overuse the words "but" and "therefore". Vary the language with phrases like:

- "The problem is..."
- "That works until..."
- "Which means..."
- "So the next job is..."
- "That solves one problem, but creates another..."
- "This is where..."

Every major beat should either create tension, explain a consequence, or resolve one problem while naturally leading to the next.

## Recommended Framework

Use a simple, owner-friendly buying-cycle framework:

- **Find**: Can customers quickly find the right product or category?
- **Trust**: Do they believe this is a real business they can safely buy from?
- **Decide**: Does the product page answer the questions and objections that block purchase?
- **Buy**: Is the cart/checkout path clear, low-friction, and asking for the sale?
- **Come back**: Is there a reason to return, reorder, review, subscribe, or buy again?

This is better for the target audience than formal language like "awareness, consideration, conversion, retention". The formal version can be mentioned briefly, but the teaching framework should stay practical.

## Pain Point

Store owners often diagnose ecommerce problems too narrowly:

- "The website needs to look better."
- "I need more traffic."
- "People are abandoning cart."
- "I need to run ads."
- "Shopify is not converting."

The deeper issue may be that the store is dropping customers at one stage of the buying cycle. Traffic cannot fix that on its own.

Examples:

- Customers arrive but cannot find the right product.
- They find a product but do not trust the business yet.
- They like the product but have unanswered questions about size, delivery, returns, ingredients, warranty, compatibility, or timing.
- They are ready to buy but the cart or checkout adds friction.
- They buy once but there is no clear post-purchase path to repeat purchase.

Another version of the same problem shows up in email and remarketing:

- A customer is still deciding, but they get a blunt discount instead of the answer they needed.
- A customer has a trust concern, but the store sends urgency.
- A customer is comparing products, but the store sends a generic abandoned cart email.
- A customer is ready to reorder, but the store has no replenishment reminder.

The right message depends on what their behaviour suggests about where they are in the buying cycle.

## Message Timing Principle

Marketing automation should support the buying cycle, not fight it.

The useful point to teach:

- If someone is in the **Find** stage, help them discover the right product or category.
- If they are in the **Trust** stage, show proof, guarantees, reviews, delivery clarity, contact options, or safety signals.
- If they are in the **Decide** stage, answer objections: size, fit, compatibility, ingredients, taste, delivery timing, returns, warranty, storage, or gifting.
- If they are in the **Buy** stage, remove friction and make checkout easy.
- If they are in the **Come Back** stage, use replenishment, education, loyalty, review requests, subscriptions, gifting reminders, or seasonal prompts.

Use a simple example in the video:

- "There is no point automatically sending every abandoned cart a discount if the reason they left was that they did not trust your delivery timing, did not understand the size, or were not sure whether the product was right for them."

This should sit early enough to frame the buying cycle as both a site problem and a messaging problem, but it should not turn the video into a Klaviyo tutorial.

Potential standalone follow-up video:

- "Why Your Abandoned Cart Email Might Be The Wrong Message"
- "How To Match Shopify Emails To The Customer Buying Cycle"
- "Stop Sending Discounts When Customers Need Answers"

## Proof Pattern

Use real, recognisable Australian ecommerce stores as proof on screen. The examples should be inspected close to recording time so the script reflects what is actually visible.

Frame proof examples carefully:

- John is not saying he is those brands.
- John is saying he works in and around that level of ecommerce through agencies and large-brand projects, so he understands the patterns and can translate them.
- The viewer should feel like they are getting access to big-brand ecommerce thinking without agency overhead or jargon.
- The irony to highlight: small stores should have the human advantage, but big brands often create the more personable online experience because they deliberately design for reassurance.

Chosen proof stores:

- **The Good Guys**: trust, safety, support, delivery/pickup, finance/payment confidence, product discovery, and reassurance for considered purchases. This gives us a JB Hi-Fi-like category without repeating the exact brand from the mobile video.
- **Koala**: strong objection handling, trial/warranty/delivery confidence, sticky buying action, and clear purchase reassurance. It is fine to reuse because it does a lot of things right.
- **Wine/alcohol/premium foodstuff brand to choose after inspection**: use this to make the guide relevant to clients in wine, alcohol, premium food, gifting, hamper, pantry, or local-producer categories.

Candidate wine/alcohol/premium food proof stores to inspect:

- **Dan Murphy's**: broad alcohol catalogue, search/discovery, delivery/pickup, price/value cues, trust and compliance expectations.
- **BWS**: convenience, local pickup/delivery, product discovery, promos, and repeat purchase.
- **Vinomofo**: wine discovery, offer framing, urgency/value, education, and trust.
- **Naked Wines Australia**: wine discovery, membership-style buying, producer story, and repeat purchase.

## Concept-To-Example Map

### Find

Question: can the customer quickly get to the right product?

Good proof targets:

- Search visibility.
- Navigation and category structure.
- Filters and sorting.
- Popular categories or guided discovery.

Likely examples:

- The Good Guys for search-led discovery and category navigation.
- Dan Murphy's, BWS, Vinomofo, or the selected premium food/wine store for filtering, occasion-led discovery, region/type/category navigation, or product education.

Practical takeaway:

- If you have lots of products, do not hide search.
- If customers need to narrow by size, type, concern, use case, brand, or budget, make that easy.

### Trust

Question: does the customer believe this is a real business and a safe purchase?

Good proof targets:

- Contact options.
- Reviews.
- Delivery/returns visibility.
- Payment options.
- Brand proof, store locations, help centre, guarantees.

Likely examples:

- The Good Guys for trust through store presence, support paths, delivery/pickup, warranties, returns, and payment confidence.
- Koala for trial, warranty, and delivery confidence.
- The selected wine/alcohol/premium food store for age/compliance expectations, provenance, delivery confidence, freshness, storage, or premium-brand trust.

Practical takeaway:

- A small Shopify store must work harder than a famous brand to prove it is real.
- Trust markers should appear before the customer is asked to pay.

### Decide

Question: does the page answer the things customers need answered before buying?

Good proof targets:

- Product details.
- Sizing, ingredients, compatibility, usage, dimensions, delivery timing.
- Reviews and Q&A.
- Comparison or recommendation cues.
- Clear explanation of value.

Likely examples:

- The Good Guys for considered-purchase decision support such as specs, reviews, warranty, delivery, and price confidence.
- Koala for trial/warranty/delivery objections.
- The selected wine/alcohol/premium food store for taste/profile, provenance, pairing, gifting, delivery timing, storage, freshness, or subscription/replenishment objections.

Practical takeaway:

- Every category has different objections.
- The job is to predict the questions customers would ask in a physical store and answer them on the product page.

### Buy

Question: once the customer is ready, is buying easy?

Good proof targets:

- Add to cart placement.
- Sticky add to cart.
- Express payment.
- Delivery/pickup clarity.
- Cart clarity.
- Checkout friction.

Likely examples:

- The Good Guys for purchase action, delivery/pickup clarity, payment options, and reassurance.
- Koala for sticky buying area and major reassurance near the CTA.
- The selected wine/alcohol/premium food store for cart, delivery threshold, pickup/delivery choice, gifting, or compliance friction.

Practical takeaway:

- Once customers are ready, do not make them hunt for the next step.
- Ask for the sale clearly.

### Come Back

Question: what happens after the first purchase?

Good proof targets:

- Subscription/replenishment.
- Loyalty.
- Email capture with a useful reason.
- Account benefits.
- Review requests.
- Post-purchase education.

Likely examples:

- Koala where relevant for post-purchase confidence, support, reviews, and referrals rather than frequent repeat purchase.
- The selected wine/alcohol/premium food store for repeat purchase, replenishment, subscription, seasonal campaigns, clubs, gifting reminders, or loyalty.
- The Good Guys for warranty, service, add-ons, and future category purchase rather than frequent replenishment.

Practical takeaway:

- Ecommerce does not end at checkout.
- If the product is replenishable, giftable, seasonal, or part of a routine, the store should make the next purchase easier.
- Email/SMS flows should match behaviour. The platform matters less than whether the message fits the customer's stage in the buying cycle.

## Commercial Intent

This is an education-led, mid-funnel guide.

It should help store owners realise ecommerce performance is not just design, traffic, or ads. It is a sequence of sales moments. That naturally leads into the Fix-It Call because many owners will need help identifying the stage where their store is losing customers and deciding what to fix first.

Commercial bridge:

- If you understand the buying cycle, you can audit your Shopify store more clearly.
- If one stage is weak, a focused Fix-It Call can help diagnose that part of the store and prioritise fixes.

## Fix-It Call Connection

Position the Fix-It Call as practical help for store owners who can see the issue but are unsure what to do next.

Suggested CTA language:

- "If you want a second pair of eyes on where your Shopify store is losing people in the buying cycle, book a Fix-It Call and we will work through it together."
- "We will focus on one part of the journey, identify what is blocking customers, and either fix what is safe in the session or leave you with a clear priority list."

Avoid making it sound like a broad sales consultation. Keep it tied to practical Shopify improvements.

## Risks Or Claims To Avoid

- Do not imply every store needs a complex funnel or heavy marketing automation.
- Do not frame ecommerce as mysterious or make small business owners feel stupid.
- Do not overpromise that understanding the buying cycle alone will fix conversion.
- Do not turn the guide into a generic marketing funnel lecture.
- Do not foreground invoiced follow-on hours; that remains post-call/customer-success context.
- Avoid saying traffic is never the problem. The point is that traffic cannot compensate for a broken buying journey.
- Do not assert that a brand is currently doing something specific unless it is checked during recording/script prep.
