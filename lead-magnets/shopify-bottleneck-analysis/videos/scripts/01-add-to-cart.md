- Cost of inaction: traffic, ads, and discounts keep sending people into a page that is not doing enough selling.
- Fix path: inspect the product page, above-the-fold promise, mobile layout, trust, imagery, price support, and traffic match.
- Paid next step: use the diagnostic to inspect the actual store and identify the real cause.

## Likely Causes

- Weak product imagery.
- Unclear value proposition.
- Price feels unsupported.
- Shipping, returns, sizing, or trust information is missing.
- Mobile layout buries the buying reasons.
- Traffic is too broad or low intent.

## First Checks

- Product page above the fold.
- Main image and gallery.
- Product title and first paragraph.
- Reviews and social proof.
- Delivery and returns clarity.
- Variant and size selection.

## Next Action

Fix the product page before obsessing over checkout or abandoned carts.

### 0:00-0:20 - Hook And Immediate Reveal

[Presenter on camera. Lower third: "Product page / add-to-cart bottleneck". Remotion side panel shows the funnel rail: sessions, add to carts, reached checkout, completed purchases.]

Before you ran this report, I would not be surprised if you thought checkout was the problem. Most store owners do.

But your result is pointing earlier than checkout: people are not adding to cart in the first place.

So the question is not, "why are they not finishing?"

It is, "why are they not even starting?"

[Remotion: checkout stage dims. Add-to-cart stage highlights. Visitor dots arrive at the product page and drop away before cart.]

That is the important thing in this result. The leak is happening before people have shown real buying intent.

### 0:20-1:15 - Problem

When the add-to-cart stage is the bottleneck, it usually means the store is getting people to look, but not getting enough of them to commit.

They may be landing on a product page. They may be browsing. They may even like the idea of the product.

But something is stopping them before they take the first real buying action.

And this is why checkout can be such a distracting place to start. Checkout only matters after someone has decided, "yes, I am interested enough to put this in my cart."

If not enough people are doing that, the first job is not abandoned checkout emails. It is not payment methods. It is not a checkout redesign.

The first job is to make the offer clearer, more believable, and easier to act on.

[Remotion: generic product page mockup appears. Above-the-fold area gets highlighted: image, title, price, promise, reviews, delivery/returns, add-to-cart button.]

### 1:15-2:00 - Recognition Story

This often feels confusing from inside the store, because you can see traffic coming in.

You might have ads running. You might have product pages that look fine. You might have a decent product, decent photos, maybe even some reviews.

But the orders are not following.

So you start looking at the obvious suspects. Maybe checkout is broken. Maybe shipping is too expensive. Maybe you need a discount code. Maybe the abandoned cart flow is not aggressive enough.

But this result is saying: slow down. Many of those people are not becoming cart abandoners at all.

They are leaving before they ever reach that point.

That changes the diagnosis.

[Remotion: "Cart abandoner" label appears, then splits into "Never reached cart" and "Reached cart". The "Never reached cart" path highlights.]

### 2:00-2:50 - Cost Of Inaction

The expensive part of an add-to-cart bottleneck is that almost every other tactic gets less efficient.

If you buy more traffic, more people hit the same hesitation.

If you run discounts, you may give away margin before you have fixed the reason people were not convinced.

If you spend time tuning checkout, you may improve a step that most of these shoppers are not reaching.

The exact dollar figure depends on your traffic and average order value, and the report below this video will show your numbers.

But the pattern is the main point: the store is losing people before the buying journey has properly started.

[Remotion: traffic arrow feeds more visitor dots into the product page. Dots continue dropping before cart. Label: "More traffic into the same leak".]

### 2:50-4:05 - Fix Path

So what do you check first?

Start above the fold on the product page, especially on mobile.

Can a shopper understand what the product is, who it is for, and why it is worth the price without hunting around?

Look at the main image and gallery. Are they showing the product clearly, in use, and at the level of detail needed to make a decision?

Look at the product title and the first paragraph. Are they just naming the item, or are they making the value obvious?

Look at reviews, delivery, returns, sizing, variants, and trust cues. Not because every product page needs to be stuffed with badges, but because shoppers need enough confidence to take the next step.

And check the add-to-cart action on mobile. Sometimes the buying reasons are buried below the fold, the button is not obvious enough, or variant selection creates more friction than it should.

[Remotion: cause checklist appears one card at a time: "Above the fold", "Gallery", "Value clarity", "Price support", "Trust", "Delivery/returns", "Mobile CTA", "Traffic match".]

There is one more thing to keep in mind: this can also be a traffic-quality issue.

If the visitors are too broad, too cold, or coming from a message that does not match the product page, the page may look like the problem when the real issue is the handoff between the ad, the landing page, and the offer.

So the product page is where we start, but we do not inspect it in isolation.

That is where the Shopify Fix-It Diagnostic comes in.

The report tells us where to look first. In your case, the first place to inspect is the product page and the buying moment before add to cart.

On the diagnostic, we would look at the actual store: the highest-volume product pages, the mobile layout, the offer clarity, the first-screen buying reasons, the trust and delivery information, the variant flow, and the traffic source if that is part of the problem.

The goal is not to throw a generic product page checklist at the store.

The goal is to work out what is actually stopping your shoppers from taking the first buying step, then turn that into a prioritised action plan.
