## Purpose

Explain why visitors are not becoming interested enough to add to cart, and why the product page needs to match the promise, build trust, and make ownership feel obvious before obsessing over checkout fixes.

## Core Message

"If people are not adding to cart, the product page has to match the promise, build trust, and make clicking Add to Cart feel inevitable."

## Diagnostic Arc

- Immediate reveal: the leak is happening before people have shown real buying intent.
- Diagnosis: the store may be getting visitors, but the product page is not yet making the buyer trust the offer and imagine owning the product.
- Cost of inaction: traffic, ads, and discounts keep sending people into a page that is not doing enough selling.
- Fix path: inspect the promise handoff, the first few seconds of the product page, trust, comfort, and the feeling of ownership.
- Paid next step: position John as the missing ecommerce conversion expertise on the merchant's team, then invite them to book a call.
- Close: everything is fixable; click the button below the video and book in a call.

## Likely Causes

- The ad, email, search result, or social post creates one expectation, but the product page pays off another.
- The first few seconds of the product page do not answer: "am I in the right place?"
- The product page explains the product but does not help the buyer imagine owning it.
- Images, copy, proof, delivery details, returns, price, or trust cues fail to make the buyer feel comfortable.
- The page makes Add to Cart feel like a risk instead of the natural next step.

## First Checks

- Within the first few seconds, does the buyer know they are in the right place?
- Does the product page pay off the promise that brought them there?
- Does the page make the product feel like the thing they were looking for?
- Does the page answer why they should buy this product from this store?
- Do the images, copy, proof, delivery details, returns, and price create trust and imagined ownership?

## Next Action

Fix the product page and offer before obsessing over checkout or abandoned carts.

That is the reveal: before we worry about abandoned carts or checkout, we need to understand why visitors are not committing to the product in the first place.

### 0:20-1:15 - Problem

When add-to-cart is the bottleneck, the problem is pretty simple:

People are looking, but they're not committing.

They're on your landing page. They're seeing the product. They might even like it.

But they're not taking that first buying action.

And that's why concentrating on checkout here is a bit of a trap.

Checkout only matters if someone's already said, "yes, this product needs to go in my cart."

If they're not doing that, abandoned checkout emails, updated payment buttons, or even a whole checkout redesign won't fix the actual issue.

We need to go back earlier in the buying cycle.

What does this customer need to hear, see, or believe before they say, "yes, this is something I want to buy"?

Because at this point, they might want the product.

They just might not want it from you yet.

That's the real problem. It's not the checkout, it's the offer.

[Remotion: generic product page mockup appears. Overlay: "Looking isn't committing". Above-the-fold area gets highlighted: image, title, price, promise, reviews, delivery/returns, add-to-cart button.]

### 1:15-2:05 - Cost Of Inaction

Now, this is already costing you money.

To see roughly how much, check out the dollar figure at the bottom of your report.

Now, there's a tricky thing about an add-to-cart bottleneck: almost every tactic you reach for can end up costing you even more money.

If you buy more traffic, more people hit the same hesitation.

If you run discounts, you're just giving away margin.

If you tune the checkout, you're spending time and money on a step that most shoppers aren't even reaching.

And that's why we focus on the offer.

But what does that even mean?

The offer is everything on that product page that moves your buyer closer to clicking Add to Cart.

The product, the promise, the price, the images, the proof, the trust signals, the delivery details, your returns policy, and even the Add to Cart button itself.

Every element on that page either moves them closer to yes, or gives them a reason to leave.

And remember, your product page only has one job: compelling the user to click Add to Cart.

[Remotion: report revenue estimate card appears, then traffic, discount, and checkout cards stack up as "more cost". Transition into a generic product page with every element between headline and add-to-cart button highlighted as "the offer".]

### 2:05-3:20 - Fix Path

So what do you check first?

Start with the first screen of the product page, especially on mobile.

Within the first few seconds, they need to understand three things:

Am I in the right place?

Is this what I'm after?

Why should I buy this from you?

Now let me explain that first one, because this is super common, but almost nobody talks about it.

There needs to be continuity between what they clicked and where they ended up.

If the ad promises one thing, but your product page delivers something else, that dissonance creates uncertainty.

Uncertainty removes trust, and once the customer loses trust, they leave.

So check the handoff first. Does the first screen of the product page pay off the promise that brought them there?

[Remotion: promise handoff split screen. Ad/message card on the left, product page first screen on the right. Overlay: "Does the page pay off the click?" Trust meter dips if the two do not match.]

The next two questions are: is this what I'm after?

And why should I buy this product from you?

Remember, the number one goal here is to get the customer to click Add to Cart.

And for that to happen, you need to put a picture in their head of already owning the product.

That's the goal of everything on the product page: the images, the copy, the proof, the delivery details, and everything else.

Everything should make the customer feel more comfortable, increase trust, and make it harder to imagine your product not being in their life.

Because once they own the product in their head, they have to click Add to Cart.

That's just the obvious next step.

[Remotion: ownership builder mockup. Images, copy, proof, delivery, returns, price, and Add to Cart light up as one connected path. Overlay: "Make owning it feel obvious."]

So that's the fix path. Match the promise that brought them in, build trust, create that feeling of ownership, and make the next step of clicking Add to Cart feel inevitable.
