# Shopify Bottleneck Analysis Video Hooks

## Review Goal

Review these hooks for tone, accuracy, and whether they create enough dissonance to make the viewer keep watching.

Each result-video hook should do four things in the first 10-20 seconds:

1. **Belief**: state what the viewer probably believed before seeing the report.
2. **Reversal**: turn that belief over using their result.
3. **Curiosity gap**: open a question without answering it yet.
4. **Bridge**: point into the diagnostic explanation.

A hook should make the viewer think: "Yes, I did believe that... wait, if that is not the problem, what is?"

The curiosity gap is the missing "why", not the result itself. The result page can already tell them the flagged stage. The hook should reveal the stage, then make them want the diagnosis behind it.

Do not answer the hook inside the hook. Name the stage, create the open loop, then use the diagnosis section to explain what is likely happening. If a hook lists likely causes, first checks, or fixes, it has failed.

Use this test:

- **Pass:** the viewer thinks, "Hang on, if that is true, what is causing it?"
- **Fail:** the viewer thinks, "Okay, I know the answer now."

The setup video is different from the result videos. The user has already clicked the ad, signed up, opened the report page, and decided they want the result. This video should not sell, agitate, or introduce anxieties they do not have. It should simply orient them and get them through the steps.

## Drafting Notes

- Hooks should feel specific to the report state, not like generic ecommerce advice.
- The setup video hook should quickly orient the user and tell them what to do next.
- The result video hooks should create dissonance without overstating the report.
- The belief and reversal must land in the first 20 seconds.
- Each result hook should lead to one bottleneck and one paid next step.
- The best hook should make the viewer feel seen and slightly surprised.

## 1. How To Get Your Report Details

Purpose: help the user follow the report page process: paste their Shopify Admin URL, open the generated report links, export/upload the funnel CSV, enter AOV, and submit.

### Opening Options

1. "Follow along with this page open. Over the next five minutes, we’re going to grab the data we need from Shopify so we can get you the most useful result possible. There’s nothing too technical here, and I’ll walk you through it step by step. You’ve got this. Let’s get started."

2. "You’re at the report step now. I’ll show you how to connect this page to your Shopify Admin, export the funnel report, upload the CSV, add average order value, and get your results."

3. "Let’s get your results. First we’ll paste your Shopify Admin URL, then open the funnel report from this page, export the CSV, upload it back here, add AOV, and run the analysis."

4. "This is the walkthrough for Part 2 of the analysis. I’ll show you what to click in Shopify, what to export, where to upload it, and how to submit the report."

5. "Follow along with this page open. In this step we’re just going to grab the data we need from Shopify, add your average order value, and then we’ll head into the results page so you can see where the biggest leak is likely happening."

### Recommended Opening

Option 5.

Why: it keeps the setup video practical and reassuring, while making the next outcome clear: submit the Shopify data and get the likely leak.

## 2. Product Page / Add-To-Cart Bottleneck

Purpose: explain why visitors are not becoming interested enough to add to cart.

### Hook Options

1. "Before you ran this report, I would not be surprised if you thought checkout was the problem. Most store owners do. But your result is pointing earlier than checkout: people are not adding to cart in the first place. So the question is not, 'why are they not finishing?' It is, 'why are they not even starting?'"

2. "A lot of merchants come into this thinking, 'I have a checkout problem.' But if your biggest leak is add to cart, checkout is not the first suspect. The report is pulling us back to the first moment of commitment, and that changes the investigation."

3. "You might have expected the report to tell you people were abandoning cart. But this result is saying many shoppers are leaving before there is a cart to abandon. That is a different problem, and it starts earlier than most stores look."

4. "The obvious answer might be, 'I need more traffic.' But when add to cart is the bottleneck, more traffic can just send more visitors into the same hesitation. Before you spend more to get people in, we need to work out what they are not seeing when they arrive."

5. "It is easy to assume people are not buying because they got scared in checkout. But your report is saying the hesitation starts before checkout even exists. That is useful, because it tells us the first real question is happening on the page, not at the payment step."

### Recommended Hook

Option 1.

Why: it uses the most common merchant belief, "checkout is the problem," then turns it over with the add-to-cart result.

## 3. Cart / Reach-Checkout Bottleneck

Purpose: explain why people add to cart but do not continue to checkout.

### Hook Options

1. "You might look at add-to-carts and think, 'Good, the product page is working.' And partly, it is. But your report is showing too many shoppers add to cart and then do not even reach checkout. So what happens in those few seconds between interest and the next step?"

2. "Most people treat cart abandonment like the customer simply changed their mind. But if shoppers add to cart and do not reach checkout, the report is pointing to a much smaller moment. They said 'maybe', then stopped before the next step. Why?"

3. "You might have expected the checkout to be the weak point. But this result says many shoppers are not even getting there. They showed interest, then stalled before checkout, which means the interesting question sits in the handoff."

4. "The easy assumption is that people need a discount to continue. But if the leak is between cart and checkout, a discount may be solving the wrong problem. Before you give away margin, we need to know what made the next click feel less obvious."

5. "If people add to cart, it is tempting to say, 'They wanted it, then they bailed.' But this result is more specific than that. They wanted it enough to take the first step, then stopped before checkout. The gap is small, but it can tell us a lot."

### Recommended Hook

Option 2.

Why: it challenges the idea that cart abandonment is just the customer changing their mind, then opens the question around the cart moment.

## 4. Checkout Completion Bottleneck

Purpose: explain why people reach checkout but do not complete the purchase.

### Hook Options

1. "You might assume that once someone reaches checkout, the hard part is basically done. But your report is showing the leak right there, near the finish line. These shoppers were close enough to buy, so the real question is: what changed at the final step?"

2. "A lot of store owners think checkout is just the final technical step. But for the customer, checkout is the final decision moment. If this is your bottleneck, the report is asking one uncomfortable question: what made paying feel harder than leaving?"

3. "You may have expected the report to show a product-page problem. But this result says the product and cart did enough to get people to checkout. The leak is later, after the store has already earned a real shot at the sale."

4. "It is easy to blame abandoned checkout on people being distracted. Sometimes they are. But when checkout completion is the main bottleneck, we cannot just write it off as distraction. We need to know what the final step is doing to the decision."

5. "Most funnel leaks are frustrating, but this one is especially expensive. You already earned the visit, the product interest, the cart action, and the checkout start. So why does the funnel lose people when buying intent should be highest?"

### Recommended Hook

Option 5.

Why: it makes the commercial cost of a checkout leak obvious without prematurely explaining the cause.

## 5. Healthy / No Major Bottleneck

Purpose: explain that the funnel is broadly okay, but there is still room to improve.

### Hook Options

1. "You might expect a healthy result to mean, 'Great, there is nothing to fix.' But that is not quite right. It means the obvious leak is not showing up in the core funnel. So the question changes from 'what is broken?' to 'what is actually worth improving?'"

2. "This is the result most people want, but it creates a different problem. If nothing obvious is broken, random optimisation gets dangerous. So what do you touch when the main funnel is already doing its job?"

3. "You might have come into this expecting the report to find a leak. But if the funnel is broadly healthy, the report is telling us something else. The opportunity may not be a dramatic fix, which means the next move is less obvious."

4. "A healthy funnel can feel like the end of the analysis. But it is actually the start of a prioritisation question. If the core funnel is not obviously broken, where should the next hour or dollar go?"

5. "The trap with a healthy result is thinking every optimisation idea is equally valid. It is not. If the funnel is broadly working, the wrong change can waste time or break something. So the next question is which lever has earned attention."

### Recommended Hook

Option 2.

Why: it keeps the result positive, but opens a useful prioritisation question instead of implying there is nothing to do.

## Review Checklist

Please review:

- Are the recommended hooks the right ones, or should any video use a different option?
- Is the tone direct enough without sounding too harsh?
- Do the result hooks create belief/reversal dissonance quickly enough?
- Do the hooks make the merchant think, "Yes, I did think that... so what is actually going on?"
- Should the result videos lean more toward revenue loss, operator frustration, or practical first checks?
- Is "Shopify Fix-It Diagnostic" still the preferred offer name for the CTA pass?
